Middle school · Evolution

Same DNA alphabet, amazing variety of life

Evolution is change in inherited traits in populations over generations. Genetic variation—differences in DNA between individuals—gives natural selection something to work with. No variation, no adaptation story.

Raw material

Variation in populations

Members of the same species are alike enough to interbreed, but they’re not genetically identical (except clones or identical twins). Mutation, recombination, and random mating all introduce and shuffle differences.

Mechanism

Natural selection

In a given environment, some traits help individuals survive and reproduce more often. Those traits become more common over time—not because organisms “try” to evolve, but because helpful alleles get passed down more frequently.

Classic example: darker moths in sooty industrial areas—camouflage changed survival odds. The environment selects phenotypes; DNA variation provides options.

Outcome

Adaptation

An adaptation is a trait that helps an organism survive and reproduce in its environment—often shaped by selection over many generations. What’s “adaptive” depends on place and time: the same allele might help or hurt if the climate shifts.

Big picture

From differences to new species

When populations are separated or preferences change, they can accumulate so many genetic differences that they no longer interbreed freely—speciation. The tree of life reflects billions of years of this process, all using the same A–T–G–C chemistry.

Review

Flashcards

What is natural selection?

A process where environmental differences change which traits reproduce more often in a population over time.

Does evolution “want” a species to get smarter?

No goals—just differential survival and reproduction of existing variation.

Why is genetic variation important?

Without differences, no allele frequencies can change in response to selection.

Check yourself

Quick quiz

1. Natural selection acts on…

Selection changes allele frequencies across generations—population-level process.

2. A helpful trait spreads in a population mainly because…

Heritable traits linked to more offspring become more common.

3. Which is a source of new genetic variation?

Mutation creates new alleles; other processes reshuffle existing variation.

4. Two populations no longer interbreed—what might have happened?

Many mechanisms—geographic barriers, behavior, timing—can split gene pools.
