Middle school · Heredity

Why do traits run in families?

You might share your grandmother’s smile or your parent’s hair texture. Heredity is how genetic information passes from parents to offspring—without copying the whole story cell-by-cell in words, DNA does it with genes and rules we can model.

Building blocks

Genes, alleles, and chromosomes

A gene is a stretch of DNA that often codes for a protein or trait. You inherit two copies of most genes—one from each parent. Each version is an allele (like different “spellings”).

Together, your alleles across all your genes make up your genotype. The traits you actually show (when they can be seen) are part of your phenotype—influenced by genes and also by environment.

Patterns

Dominant and recessive

For many simple traits, one allele can “hide” another. A dominant allele shows its effect if you have one or two copies. A recessive allele only shows if you have two copies—because there’s no dominant version to mask it.

Example: In pea plants, tall (T) is dominant over short (t). A plant with TT or Tt is tall; only tt is short.

Real human traits are usually more complex—many genes and the environment matter—but the idea of dominant/recessive is a useful starting model.

Prediction

Punnett squares — chance in a grid

A Punnett square predicts offspring genotypes when you know parents’ alleles. Each parent passes one allele at random for that gene (for simple models).

If both parents are Tt (heterozygous tall), the square shows 25% TT, 50% Tt, 25% tt—so 75% tall phenotype, 25% short.

Beyond DNA

Genes aren’t the whole story

Nutrition, sunlight, practice, and health all shape how traits appear. Height has a genetic component but also depends on diet and care. DNA sets possibilities; life experiences help decide how they play out.

Review

Flashcards

Tap a question to reveal a short answer.

What is an allele?

A version of a gene—like different spellings of the same instruction.

What is a genotype?

The combination of alleles you carry for a gene (e.g. Tt).

What is a phenotype?

The observable trait—what we can measure or see, shaped by genes and environment.

Why can two tall pea plants still have a short child?

If both are Tt, there’s a 25% chance each offspring is tt (short).

Check yourself

Quick quiz

1. You inherit one allele for a gene from each…

You get one copy from your biological mother and one from your biological father (for autosomal genes).

2. In simple models, a recessive trait shows when an organism is…

Two recessive alleles (e.g. aa)—no dominant allele to mask them.

3. A Punnett square helps you predict…

It organizes how parental alleles can combine—under simple inheritance assumptions.

4. Phenotype depends on…

Many traits are multifactorial: DNA + life experience + sometimes luck.
