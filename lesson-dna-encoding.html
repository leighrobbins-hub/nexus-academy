Flashcards — tap a card to reveal the answer

Definition

What are the four DNA bases, and who pairs with whom?

A, T, G, C — Adenine pairs with Thymine; Guanine pairs with Cytosine (A–T, G–C).
Definition

What is a codon?

A codon is a group of three bases that usually codes for one amino acid or a stop signal.
Definition

How many standard amino acids build most proteins?

20 standard amino acids—like a 20-letter “protein alphabet” built from 4 DNA letters in triplets.
Concept

What does mRNA do in the story of a gene?

mRNA carries a copy of the code from DNA to the ribosome, using U instead of T.
Concept

What is the central dogma?

DNA → RNA → Protein: information flows from genes to RNA messages to proteins.
Application

Real-world analogy: DNA is like what?

A recipe book in the nucleus—each gene is a recipe; the cell reads it to cook the right proteins.
Definition

Why is the genetic code called “degenerate”?

Multiple codons can specify the same amino acid—more words than amino acids.
Concept

What happens if one base in a gene is copied wrong?

That’s a mutation—it may change an amino acid, the protein’s shape, or sometimes almost nothing.
Application

Why can DNA identify a person?

Except identical twins, people differ in many variable DNA regions—enough to match one genome to one person.

Go deeper

Why order matters — and what can go wrong

You’ve seen the ladder and the letters. Here’s how scientists think about the same ideas in stories, mistakes, and real life.

Recipe book — Think of the whole genome as a giant cookbook. Each gene is one recipe (a stretch of bases). The order of letters in that stretch is the exact order of steps: which amino acids to string together to make a working protein.
Example — Changing a codon from GAA (glutamic acid) to GTA (valine) in the hemoglobin gene causes sickle-shaped red blood cells in sickle cell disease— one letter swap, big change in the protein’s behavior.
Mutations — Not every typo breaks the protein. Some swaps hit a redundant codon (same amino acid). Others create a stop signal early or a totally wrong amino acid— effects range from harmless to serious.
Why this matters — Same logic powers medicine (genetic tests, gene therapy research), forensics (DNA fingerprinting), and evolution (mutations + selection = diversity over time).

    Checkpoint quiz

    Four questions — instant feedback

    No pressure: pick an answer, see green/red, then read a short why. Your score fills the Quiz bar in the top bar.

    1. In DNA, which base pairs with adenine (A) on the opposite strand?

    A pairs with T in DNA. (RNA uses U instead of T when the message is read.)

    2. A gene is transcribed from DNA: TAC-GCA-AAT. Which mRNA segment matches?

    Transcription pairs T→A, A→U, C→G, G→C. TAC→AUG, GCA→CGU, AAT→UUA.

    3. A mutation changes one base in a codon. Will the protein always change?

    The genetic code is redundant: different codons can specify the same amino acid, so some single-base changes are “silent.”

    4. Why can scientists use DNA sequences to help identify individuals?

    Non-coding and other variable regions differ between people—enough to compare profiles in forensics and paternity tests.
